Assignment 4 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Rayyan Khalid — rkhalid@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Complaints against NYPD officers are less likely to be substantiated when the officer is white.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Y-axis truncation (-1.0): The axis starts at 15% instead of 0%, exaggerating visual differences to make gaps between groups seem larger.
  • Color emphasis (-0.5): The White officer bar is colored bright red to immediately draw viewer attention and suggest urgency.
  • Title framing (-1.5): The emotionally charged title “White Officers Least Likely to Face Accountability” frames the chart toward a strong interpretation.
  • Straightforward encoding (+2.0): Simple vertical bar chart makes it easy for readers to compare across racial groups without confusion.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Alternative explanation (+2.0): Introduced officer rank as a confounding factor, offering a different framing for accountability differences.
  • Direct comparison (+1.5): Side-by-side bars allow viewers to quickly compare substantiation rates across ranks for white and Black officers.
  • Neutral color encoding (+2.0): Used shades of gray to avoid drawing unnecessary attention to any particular group.
  • Clear labeling (+2.0): Axis labels and percentages are concise and easy to read, minimizing reader confusion.

Final Reflection

This project helped me realize how powerful even small visualization decisions can be in shaping the narrative. My proposition - that white NYPD officers are less likely to have complaints substantiated - was both controversial and data-rich, making it ideal for persuasive visualization.

Designing the "FOR" visualization taught me how simple techniques like axis manipulation and color emphasis can dramatically affect a viewer's perception, even when using truthful data. Meanwhile, designing the "AGAINST" visualization reminded me that introducing broader context, like officer rank, leads to a more nuanced and fairer story. I now believe that ethical visualization isn’t just about avoiding outright lies - it’s about how openly we frame the story and whether we allow viewers to see multiple interpretations instead of just one. Transparency about design intent is key.

I found that supporting the proposition was more emotionally powerful, especially with visual tactics like color emphasis and truncated axes. Refuting the proposition, on the other hand, required more analytical framing: introducing alternate variables like complaint type and rank helped provide context. While Set A leaned into rhetorical techniques, Set B felt more fair and transparent - but perhaps less persuasive. This tension made me rethink what qualifies as ethical data storytelling.

Ultimately, I've come to define ethical visualization not just as accurate encoding, but also as transparency about intent. Persuasion is not inherently deceptive, but it becomes problematic when viewers can't tell how or why they're being nudged toward a conclusion.
