Assignment 4 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Rayyan Khalid — rkhalid@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Complaints against NYPD officers are less likely to be substantiated when the officer is white.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Y-axis truncation (-1.0): The axis starts at 15% instead of 0%, exaggerating the visual gap in substantiation rates.
  • Color emphasis (-0.5): Red bar used for white officers to draw attention and suggest alarm or urgency.
  • Title framing (-1.5): The title “White Officers Least Likely to Face Accountability” is emotionally charged and slanted.
  • Straightforward encoding (+2.0): Simple bar chart helps readers immediately grasp racial disparity.

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Line emphasis (-1.0): White officer line is solid and thick, while others are dotted to emphasize contrast.
  • Annotations (+0.5): Events like “Body cam rollout” are used to imply external influence, guiding interpretation.
  • Data filtering (-1.5): Only the full trend for white officers is shown; early data for others omitted for clarity.
  • Color encoding (+1.0): Uses color hierarchy (red > gray) to lead the eye subtly.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Control variable (+1.5): Complaint type used to show the outcome varies more by complaint category than by officer race.
  • Neutral color encoding (+2.0): Uses a muted grayscale palette to avoid implying bias.
  • Balanced layout (+2.0): Symmetrical grouped bars support direct, fair comparisons.

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Alternative explanation (+2.0): Introduces officer rank as a possible confounding variable.
  • Direct comparison (+1.5): Renders white and Black officer data side-by-side for all ranks to keep focus clear.
  • Clear labeling (+2.0): Uses concise rank titles and easy-to-read percentages to maintain clarity.

Final Reflection

This project challenged my assumptions about data neutrality and helped me see how easily visual design choices can shape perception. My proposition - that white NYPD officers are less likely to have complaints substantiated - was both controversial and data-rich, making it ideal for persuasive visualization.

I found that supporting the proposition was more emotionally powerful, especially with visual tactics like color emphasis and truncated axes. Refuting the proposition, on the other hand, required more analytical framing: introducing alternate variables like complaint type and rank helped provide context. While Set A leaned into rhetorical techniques, Set B felt more fair and transparent - but perhaps less persuasive. This tension made me rethink what qualifies as ethical data storytelling.

Ultimately, I've come to define ethical visualization not just as accurate encoding, but also as transparency about intent. Persuasion is not inherently deceptive, but it becomes problematic when viewers can't tell how or why they're being nudged toward a conclusion.
